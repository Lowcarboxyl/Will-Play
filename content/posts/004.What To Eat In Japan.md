The classic question: Do you have any food recc’s in Japan? While yes I do, I think the best way to eat food in Japan is to choose a type of food, and then go find a place near you that serves it! The question is then, what are the genres of food in Japan? I have compiled this helpful list to help you with that exact thing.

AND STOP WITH THAT FACE. Don’t listen to them. Yes, I too love sushi and ramen, but what makes Japanese food Japanese, is that it is commonly eaten in Japan. Japanese restaurants have adopted global cuisines and adapted them into entirely new flavors, and more importantly to me, changed the culture around eating and serving it. The result is often even more Japanese than eating katsudon on a street corner in Shibuya.

One final thing about ratings in Japan. In the Japanese food rating app, Tabelog (食べログ), 3 stars is considered normal, and 3.5 and above is reserved for top 10% restaurants. I have my own picky points with that, because it has a tendency to weigh service absurdly high, and they favor high-class restaurants. But in terms of online reviews on Google, don’t trust anything—just filter by newest and see what language people use and what they are noting. Some of my favorite restaurants are 3.4-star rated with 50 reviews, and the occasional tourist restaurant doesn’t have to be bad, it’s just what it is—a highly curated restaurant that's comfortable for tourists.

15. French - フランス

French, as in, cooking style. This is highly trained restaurant owners who worked in restaurants across the world but came back to Japan to open up their own restaurant where they serve highly selective ingredients in their own recipes. They range from expensive Michelin stars to this super cute guy in a back alley who knew someone who owned a bar and decided to try their hand at their own lunch place. This is where I talked the most Japanese to full strangers.

16. Indo-Nepa - インド
<div class="gallery-slide" style="max-width: 500px; margin: 20px auto;">

    <div class="slide-caption">The naan is as big as the plate. AND ITS A BIG PLATE.</div>

Indian food by name, but actually owned by Nepalese people most of the time. Highly sweet and mild curries with gigantic, huge, friggen giant huge pieces of naan. This is surprisingly one of the most standardized menus in Japan—the equivalent of Mexican food in America—where you can be sure it's to be found in the middle-of-nowhere towns.

17. Chinese Food - 中華
<div class="gallery-slide" style="max-width: 500px; margin: 20px auto;">

    <div class="slide-caption">Sorry, I like them steamed. But they always have the egg drop soup, mapo, and almond tofu.</div>

There are actually two types of Chinese food: Chuka (中華) and Chinese (中国) food, where the first is Chinese food that's been heavily standardized as essentially Chinese food dishes that the average Japanese homestead can cook. Heavily oily, and where the super overworked salaryman goes to eat.

18. Family Restaurant - ファミレス
<div class="gallery-slide" style="max-width: 500px; margin: 20px auto;">

    <div class="slide-caption">Literally Denny's in Japan</div>

A personal favorite of mine. Super mediocre food, but with pretty spacious tables, and usually able to order by tablet. If you are ever at a loss, or have children with you, these are sure to be able to seat you, and you will have a good time. Nothing beats the rush of relaxation you get sitting at one of these tables and organizing all the busy thoughts in your head.

19. Saizeriya - サイゼリヤ
<div class="gallery-slide" style="max-width: 500px; margin: 20px auto;">
    <img src="/images/20240920 (23).jpg" alt="Description of your photo">

The best restaurant in Japan. An engineering model. All-you-can-drink red wine. Get the gratin, 4 other things on the menu, and be amazed. Or larp a poor college student and get a bowl of spaghetti and bread, and cry. All are sure to be experiences of a lifetime.

20. Local Specialties - 名物
<div class="gallery-slide" style="max-width: 500px; margin: 20px auto;">
    <img src="/images/20240920 (24).jpg" alt="Description of your photo">
    <div class="slide-caption">Soba from Nagano!</div>

Every place has the best food in Japan of something. Try it! Just look up the local specialty and find a place that serves it. Japan is engineered so that eating locally is efficient, so local foods will always be the tastiest.

21. Bonus - Ryokan - 旅館
<div class="gallery-slide" style="max-width: 500px; margin: 20px auto;">
    <img src="/images/20240920 (25).jpg" alt="Description of your photo">
    <div class="slide-caption">Spread for a king, or their servants</div>
</div>
Not only food, but a full night's stay, both dinner and breakfast, and a wonderful bath. Unmatched relaxation, only a couple of hours from the biggest cities in the world. The only way Japanese salarypeople can spend their frequent 3-day-long vacations. Feel how the richest samurai lords felt, traveling the Tokaido, spending their prefecture's net worth to send a single child to Tokyo.

There are of course, tons of other types of things to eat. I ate so much Korean and Vietnamese food in Japan, but at its core, those restaurants are pretty similar to eating it in America. There's also other types of Japanese food, like yakitori, kushiyaki, or the classic teppanyaki—what Benihana popularized in America. There's also karaage, shabushabu, and sukiyaki too, but in my opinion, those are all either highly localized or too similar to (or a worse version of) ordinary American restaurants. These 20 are the ones I think are the Japanese foods to try.

Welcome to the base of the tallest freestanding tower on Earth at its completion, and still sitting comfortably as the third-tallest structure in the world today.

And I’m sure if you have been to any other massive building project in the world, you know what to expect at its foundation: gated corporate lobbies, high-end luxury boutiques, multi-million dollar real estate offices, and fine dining targeted strictly at the top 1% of Asia’s elite.

Except… they are absolutely nowhere to be found.

Instead, if you walk up to the Skytree, you will find cute collaboration cafes, the same clothing chains and dessert shops that anchor every countryside mall in Japan, and local neighborhood grandmas parking their mamachari to grab dinner ingredients at the basement supermarket. This isn’t a gated citadel that will allow you the privilege of looking down on Tokyo from on high; rather, it was built to service a direct need for Tokyo and the surrounding community.

<div class="gallery-slide" style="max-width: 500px; margin: 20px auto;">

Skytree was actually built for the purpose of being a broadcast tower. Usually, in most other cities, the broadcast antenna is just slapped on top of the highest building, and it is able to reach every point in the city easily. However, Tokyo, in being a city with multiple major population hubs, needed a mega high point that could reach all of those major hubs. In a country prone to major earthquakes, keeping over-the-air television and radio robust is a matter of critical disaster response, and so, in July of 2008, ground was broken, construction began, and in May 2012, Tokyo Skytree was opened to the public.

However, making large functional infrastructure work well with the community requires more than just a pretty building facade. Something I personally learned while living in Tokyo is that the outskirts of Tokyo, outside of the Yamanote line, are actually surprisingly unconnected. Even now they are building tons of new subway lines, usually in an effort to either make a lateral connection from an outer suburb to another outer suburb, or building up a line so that people can have a straight shot to the city center without having to do a weird connection.

<div class="gallery-slide" style="max-width: 500px; margin: 20px auto;">
    <img src="https://lowcarboxyl.github.io/Will-Play/images/260417 (7).jpg" alt="Description of your photo">
    <div class="slide-caption">Tokyo to Haneda in 20 minutes—the American airport cannot comprehend.</div>

Case in point: originally, the Hanzomon line terminated in central Tokyo, but after 2003, they pushed the line all the way out here, to Oshiage. It suddenly connected a quiet, working-class area to the busy city centers of Shibuya and Omotesando directly without a single transfer, cutting what could be an hour ride down to 20 minutes.

If we talk about the Sumida area itself, the Oshiage and Narihirabashi districts were traditionally residential and light industry. It used to be said that this was always the cheapest area to live close to Tokyo due to it being near or below sea level, leading to high flood risk. The exact spot of Skytree used to be a former rail freight yard owned by Tobu Railway.

This railway’s presence meant that if you were coming from the north and you really wanted to get on that train, you would literally have to walk all the way around to finally get on a train to Akihabara.

And so, now that the skytower is built, and with its various entry points and layered crosswalks, it is easy to keep the tower-struck, picture-taking tourists clear and out of the way of the everyday commuters who are just trying to get to work. Even now, the basement is a normal Life supermarket, and while the floor of just dessert items may seem odd to tourists, let me tell you, it isn’t. That is the core of any local station department store, and the food there is midcost at best.

This isn’t a gatekeeping post, though. What I mean is, while many people rate Skytree as underwhelming, it is only that if you think of it as a theme park. Skytree, rather, was built as a beautiful city park—a place to go for a day's rest, where you can have a nice aquarium visit, eat dessert on the outdoor patio, and just sit and talk for a couple of hours. If a vacation is just a speedrun of getting Instagram photos, Skytree is undoubtedly low ROI, but that exact reason is how it manages to maintain its strength among locals as a prized part of the community, and will help keep it from falling into disrepair.
